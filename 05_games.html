Adversarial Search: Games

← Back

Lab Focus: Deterministic zero-sum games, Minimax decision-making, and state-space pruning.

1. Game Taxonomy

In AI, we categorize games based on the information available to players and the element of luck.

Perfect Information

Every player knows the entire history of actions. No hidden moves (e.g., Chess).

Complete Information

Players know the rules, payoffs, and incentives of all opponents. No hidden goals.

Type Deterministic (No Luck) Chance (Dice/Cards)
Perfect Information Chess, Go, Tic-Tac-Toe Monopoly, Backgammon
Incomplete Information Battleships Poker, Bridge, Scrabble

2. The Minimax Algorithm

Minimax aims to minimize the maximum possible loss. It assumes a "rational opponent" who plays optimally to maximize their own advantage.

MAX Player (You)

Attempts to choose the move that leads to the highest evaluation score.

MIN Player (Opponent)

Attempts to choose the move that leads to the lowest evaluation score.

Optimality Conditions

Minimax Recurrence: V(s) = max(min(V(successors)))
  • Zero-Sum: One player's gain is the other's exact loss.
  • Time Complexity: $O(b^m)$ where $b$ is the branching factor and $m$ is the depth.
  • Space Complexity: $O(bm)$ (Depth-first exploration).

3. Alpha-Beta Pruning

Alpha-Beta pruning is an optimization that allows us to skip branches in the game tree that cannot possibly influence the final decision.

The Pruning Rule:
If β ≤ α, then the current branch is pruned (cutoff).

• α (Alpha): The best value MAX can guarantee so far.
• β (Beta): The best value MIN can guarantee so far.
Prune when: β ≤ α

Implementation Insight

The effectiveness of pruning highly depends on Move Ordering. If the best moves are evaluated first, more branches can be eliminated early, potentially reducing complexity to $O(b^{m/2})$ in the best case.

4. Real-World Limitations

For complex games like Chess, we cannot search to terminal states. We introduce:

  • Heuristic Evaluation: Estimating the value of a board position (e.g., counting pieces).
  • Search Horizon: A fixed depth limit for the algorithm.
  • Quiescence Search: Expanding volatile positions (like captures) to avoid the "horizon effect."
