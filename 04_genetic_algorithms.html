1. Introduction

Genetic Algorithms (GA) are metaheuristic search techniques inspired by the process of natural selection. They are used to find high-quality solutions for optimization and search problems.

[Image of DNA chromosome and gene structure diagram]
Gene

A single parameter or feature of a solution (e.g., a specific bit or coordinate).

Chromosome

A complete set of genes representing one individual potential solution.

Population

The total set of chromosomes currently being evaluated.

2. The GA Lifecycle

GAs operate through a repetitive cycle of evaluation and reproduction to "evolve" better solutions over time.

2.1 Selection Mechanisms

Selection determines which individuals get to pass their genetic material to the next generation based on Selection Pressure.

Method Concept Pros Cons
Roulette Wheel Probability proportional to fitness Simple, intuitive Premature convergence
Tournament Best of a random subset wins Controllable pressure via k Potential diversity loss
Rank-Based Sorted index determines probability Prevents "super-individual" dominance Slower convergence
Elitism Best n survive automatically Preserves best solutions Reduces exploration

3. Recombination & Variation

3.1 Crossover (Recombination)

Crossover combines genetic information from two parents to create offspring. This is the primary exploration tool in GAs.

One-Point Crossover Example:
Parent 1: 1 2 3 | 4 5
Parent 2: 5 4 3 | 2 1
Child 1: 1 2 3 | 2 1
Child 2: 5 4 3 | 4 5

3.2 Mutation

Mutation involves randomly altering a gene. This maintains genetic diversity and prevents the population from getting stuck in local optima.

4. Classic Problems

  • Traveling Salesman (TSP): Finding the shortest Hamiltonian path through n cities.
  • Knapsack Problem: Selecting items with given weights and values to maximize value without exceeding capacity.
